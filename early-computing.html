Early Computing

The early computing era marks the beginning of the journey to develop modern computers. During this period, fundamental concepts and technologies were introduced, laying the groundwork for future advancements.

Key Events

  • 1822: Charles Babbage begins work on the Difference Engine.
  • 1837: Charles Babbage proposes the Analytical Engine, the first concept of a general-purpose computer.
  • 1842: Ada Lovelace writes the first algorithm intended for implementation on a machine.
  • 1936: Alan Turing introduces the concept of a Turing Machine.
  • 1941: Konrad Zuse completes the Z3, the world's first programmable computer.

Notable Figures

Charles Babbage

Known as the "father of the computer," Babbage designed the first automatic mechanical computer, the Difference Engine, and later the Analytical Engine.

Ada Lovelace

Recognized as the first computer programmer, Lovelace wrote an algorithm for the Analytical Engine to calculate Bernoulli numbers.

Alan Turing

A pioneer in theoretical computer science and artificial intelligence, Turing introduced the concept of the Turing Machine, a fundamental model of computation.

Key Inventions

Difference Engine

Designed by Charles Babbage, the Difference Engine was an automatic mechanical calculator designed to tabulate polynomial functions.

Analytical Engine

The Analytical Engine, also designed by Babbage, was a proposed mechanical general-purpose computer, a precursor to modern computers.

Turing Machine

Introduced by Alan Turing, the Turing Machine is a mathematical model of computation that defines an abstract machine.
